## Ordering principles

### The accumulative process (Leavitt)

Playing technique is an accumulative process. Each time you review previously-studied material, it feels easier. Two rules govern this:

1. **Don't try to completely perfect any one lesson before moving on.** Progress comes from revisiting, not from drilling until flawless.
2. **Do not skip or slight anything.** Every exercise exists for a reason — gaps in the sequence create gaps in the skill.

Constant, regular review is mandatory. The sequence is: **introduce → proceed → review → repeat**. Never proceed-only, never review-only.

**SlopScale implication:** Pathway tempo tiers should be designed so that a student spends time at each tier before advancing, but is not expected to "master" before seeing the next tier. The "Next Variation" button should cycle through related content (positions, keys) to facilitate review without tedium.

### Derivation-based learning (Leavitt)

New material is most efficiently learned when it is directly related to something already known. Leavitt's chord form sequence explicitly presents each new form as a modification (add/move/remove one finger) of a preceding form. This principle extends to scales:
- All non-major scales are derived from the four major scale fingering types by modifying specific notes.
- All modes are derived from the parent major scale by starting at a different degree.
- Arpeggios are derived from chord shapes by playing notes individually.

**SlopScale implication:** Pathway order should move from simple to derived-complex, not from unrelated topic to topic. E-shape major → E-shape Dorian is better than E-shape major → A-shape Lydian.

### Pentatonic before major scale (Desi Serna)

Serna explicitly argues that, from a playing perspective, guitarists learn better starting with the pentatonic scale rather than the full major scale:
- The pentatonic "box shapes" are immediately musical and usable over common chord progressions.
- The major scale's full 7-note patterns take longer to internalize before they sound good.
- The pentatonic's dual-key nature (major/minor in same shapes) teaches relative key relationships organically.

After pentatonic: blues scale (add the b5 chromatic passing tone). After blues scale: major scale in the same CAGED zone. After major scale: modes as modifications.

**SlopScale implication:** Beginner-tier pathways should start with pentatonic, not major scale. Only introduce the major scale after 5-note pentatonic patterns are fluent.

### Daily practice and discipline (Aebersold)

"Don't play things over and over that you can already play. This is great for the ego but does little to advance musical progress."

Aebersold's framework:
1. Establish a daily practice routine.
2. Practice scales, chords (arpeggios), and exercises in all twelve keys — not just comfortable ones.
3. Improvise every day — even if only using a few notes. Don't wait until everything is "ready."
4. Listen, listen, listen. Recorded music is the primary teacher. Internalize the tradition before trying to depart from it.
5. Use a play-along (rhythmic backing) as soon as possible — playing with a rhythm section accelerates learning.

**SlopScale implication:** The tool should encourage cycling through keys automatically. Don't let a pathway stay in one key indefinitely. The "Next Variation" system should include key changes as a progression variable.

### Reading studies (Leavitt)

Leavitt's reading study rule: "Do NOT practice these reading studies. Do not play them on two consecutive days. Do not go back over any section because of a wrong note. Keep an even tempo."

The purpose is to develop genuine reading ability (not memorization). By maintaining tempo over note-accuracy, the player learns to scan ahead and recognize patterns, not play from memory. The skill transfers: a player who can sight-read fluently has internalized rhythm, position, and finger choice at the pattern recognition level.

**SlopScale implication:** SlopScale is a generator, not a sight-reading tool — but the same principle applies to generated exercises. Tempo should be kept steady even through errors; the exercise should keep moving. Don't pause or loop at errors.

## Tempo progression frameworks

### Slow to fast — the only reliable path (Leavitt)

"Practice all material slowly enough to maintain an even tempo." Speed emerges from accumulated slow-tempo accuracy, not from gradually pushing faster before the pattern is solid.

The sequence: slow and even → review → slightly faster → review → repeat. Each tempo is a stopping point, not a ramp.

**Red flag:** If accuracy collapses at a given tempo, the jump was too large. Return to the previous tier and add more review time before attempting again.

### Leavitt's dexterity principle

"Speed not coming? Left-hand accuracy not consistent? Play any scale very slowly. Watch your left hand. Force your fingers to remain poised over the fretboard always in readiness. Don't let them move too far away from the strings when not in use."

Finger economy (staying close to the strings) is the bottleneck, not raw speed. The tempo progression should force attention to this.

**SlopScale implication:** Tempo tiers should have a genuine gap between them — not 5 BPM increments. A tier system like 60→80→100→120→140→160 gives meaningful threshold crossings. The gap forces the student to consolidate, not just chase a number.

### Jazz practice tempo framework (Aebersold)

For jazz improvisation:
1. Learn the exercise/lick slowly enough to play correctly.
2. Practice in all 12 keys before moving on — don't proceed until the shape is transposable.
3. Gradually increase tempo.
4. Eventually, the lick/scale/arpeggio should feel as natural as the alphabet.

Aebersold specifically warns against skipping key transposition: "Make a habit of practicing in all twelve keys. Volumes 21 and 24 are excellent [for this]."

## Common method book sequences and what they get right/wrong

### A Modern Method For Guitar (Berklee) — Leavitt

**What it gets right:**
- Accumulated technique over perfection-seeking is pedagogically correct.
- Deriving everything from 4 major scale fingering types is an elegant unified system.
- Regular complete review as a structural element (not optional) is essential.
- Reading and technique development run in parallel, each supporting the other.
- Chord forms presented in derivative sequence minimizes memorization load.

**What it doesn't address (for SlopScale purposes):**
- No explicit CAGED-system framing (Leavitt uses position numbers and fingering types, not CAGED shape names).
- No pathways or goal-oriented practice sequences — it's a linear method.
- No improvisation methodology — purely technique and notation reading.
- Arpeggio studies only in major keys (C, F, G, D, A) in Vol. 1.

### Fretboard Theory — Desi Serna

**What it gets right:**
- Pentatonic-first approach is more immediately musical for guitarists than traditional scale-first methods.
- CAGED template as a chord-scale-arpeggio unified system is the right mental model.
- Song-based learning as the "glue" that makes theory stick is a powerful pedagogical claim and is well-supported by experience.
- Five patterns must be learned by shape, not fret number — critical insight for transposition.

**What it doesn't address:**
- Tempo progression — no explicit tempo methodology.
- Jazz harmony depth (limited to popular music chord progressions).
- 3NPS (three-notes-per-string) patterns — Fretboard Theory focuses on CAGED box patterns only.

### Aebersold Jazz Handbook — Aebersold

**What it gets right:**
- Improvisation is a daily practice, not a skill achieved after mastering exercises.
- Chord tones (1, 3, 5, 7) as natural phrase anchors is a key insight with wide application.
- Repetition and sequence in solos are features, not flaws — most novices avoid repetition out of ego; this is an error.
- Playing over backing tracks as soon as possible is the fastest path to functional improvisation.
- "The goal is to play what you hear in your head" — technique serves musical imagination, not the reverse.

**What it doesn't address:**
- Guitar-specific fingering or position systems.
- CAGED shapes or fretboard navigation.
- Arpeggio/scale fingerings.

### Guitar Method: All Scales And Arpeggios

**What it gets right:**
- Comprehensive formula reference for every common chord/arpeggio type.
- "Essential tones" principle (which notes to prioritize when full formula isn't practical) is immediately useful.
- Approach patterns (1-1-1, 1-2-3, 3-2-1, 4-note variants) as practice variation tools are excellent for avoiding mechanical-sounding runs.
- All arpeggio formulas are mapped against modes ("relative modes"), tying arpeggios to their harmonic function.

**Caveats:**
- The mode/formula descriptions are inconsistent in places (e.g., melodic scale formula mislabeled, blues scale formula unusual).
- No position-based system — presents patterns from each root string position but doesn't frame in terms of CAGED or fingering types.

## Aebersold's practice procedure for learning scales to any song

This 12-step progression (from the Aebersold Jazz Handbook) is a systematic way to internalize a new scale over a chord progression — applicable to any practice tool that provides a backing track:

1. Play the **root/tonic note** of each chord in sequence (root only, whole notes)
2. Play the **first 2 notes** of each chord's scale
3. Play the **first 3 notes** of each chord's scale
4. Play the **first 5 notes** of each chord's scale (pentatonic subset)
5. Play the **triad** (1, 3, 5) of each chord's scale
6. Play **7th chords** (1, 3, 5, 7) of each chord's scale
7. Play **9th chords** (1, 3, 5, 7, 9) of each chord's scale
8. Play the **entire scale** up and down
9. Play **6th chords** (1, 3, 5, 6) of each chord's scale
10. Play the scale **up to the 9th and back down the chord tones**
11. Play **up the 9th chord and down the scale**
12. Play the scale in **broken thirds** (1-3, 2-4, 3-5, 4-6, 5-7, 6-8, etc. up and down)

**SlopScale implication:** This 12-step procedure maps directly onto a pathway "level" system. SlopScale's `SEQUENCE_PATTERNS` already handles step 12 (broken thirds) and basic scale runs (step 8). Steps 5–7 (triad → 7th → 9th) are arpeggio generators. The full 12-step sequence is a natural ordering for a "Scale Mastery" pathway.

## Aebersold's song-learning tips

1. Listen to the song repeatedly; memorize the melody in your mind before touching the instrument.
2. Play the melody slowly, copying every inflection from the recording.
3. Learn chord scales/progressions **in the order they appear in the song** — don't practice all scales separately first.
4. Emphasize 3rds and 7ths in your improvised lines over each chord.
5. Memorize melody AND chord scales; know chord tone locations on your instrument.
6. Improvise original melodies based on what your **mind hears** — the mind guides, the fingers follow.
7. Learn lyrics to instrumental songs when possible — singing the lyrics while playing the melody deepens melodic expression.

**Scale practice principle:** "Don't play things over and over that you can already play. This is great for the ego but does little to advance musical progress." — Aebersold. This is consistent with Leavitt's accumulative process — advance, review, advance.

## Structured practice methodology (Guitar Building Blocks — Stefurak)

Stefurak's approach uses **written practice logs** as the discipline mechanism. Key features applicable to SlopScale:

### Practice log structure

Each session log specifies:
- Date and time (scheduled in advance, not improvised)
- Exercise name
- **Target BPM** and subdivision (e.g., 120 BPM / 16th notes)
- **Duration goal** (e.g., 5 minutes per exercise)
- **Quality target** (how many reps at tempo without errors)

The log is written *before* the session begins. This transforms practice from "pick up guitar and noodle" into a scheduled appointment with measurable output.

### Six building blocks — weekly practice scope (Stefurak)

Stefurak's minimum viable weekly practice structure covers all six building blocks every week:

| Block | Practice vehicle |
|---|---|
| Notes on the neck | One note per day; say fret numbers aloud; visualize with eyes closed |
| Dexterity | Semi-chromatic 4 notes/string at 120 BPM 16th notes; 3 notes/string variant |
| Hammer-on / pull-off | 2 reps at tempo before switching |
| Crab exercise | Chromatic stretch across frets at 140 BPM |
| CAGED major chords | Cycle all five shapes at 120 BPM quarter notes |

**Visualization as a practice mode:** Stefurak emphasizes mental practice without the instrument — while commuting, in the shower, during breaks. Visualizing the note positions is as important as physically playing them.

### Daily note memorization technique (Stefurak)

- Learn one note per day (A Sunday → G Saturday)
- Play the note on every string (open nut to 11th fret only)
- Say the fret numbers aloud as you play (e.g., "5 0 7 2 10 5" for A across 6 strings)
- Recall with eyes closed; check against fretboard
- Revisit during the day without the instrument — car, shower, waiting in line

One week completes A through G. Repeat cycle for four weeks; all natural notes become automatic.

## Bebop practice methodology (Mastering Bebop Scales — Ghijselen)

Two practice modes for bebop scales:

### 1. Cycles (key cycling)

Practice each bebop exercise through the cycle of fourths (C → F → Bb → Eb → Ab → Db → Gb → B → E → A → D → G → C). Each key is a new repetition of the same technique. This:
- Prevents the common bad habit of practicing only in comfortable keys
- Trains the ear across all tonal centers
- Aligns with Aebersold's "practice in all twelve keys" directive

### 2. Chord changes on standards (slow → correct → tempo)

Take a jazz standard's chord changes. Apply the bebop scale exercise over one full chorus without a steady beat — focus on the technique, not the rhythm. Then add the beat slowly; increase tempo gradually. Don't chase Bird-tempo performance before the mechanics are solid.

**Patience principle (Ghijselen):** "Master each technique thoroughly before moving on. It could take you minutes, hours or months — who cares. Each step is a step forward." This is consistent with Leavitt's "don't try to perfectly master before proceeding" but adds a critical nuance: *the mechanic* should be clean before increasing tempo, even if perfect performance at tempo isn't required before moving to the next concept.

## Practice implications for SlopScale

1. **Pathway sequence: pentatonic → blues → major → modes → arpeggios → 7th arpeggios.** This aligns with Serna's order and Leavitt's derivative principle. Each step adds one concept to what's already known.

2. **All twelve keys, eventually.** No pathway should be considered complete until it's been practiced in all twelve keys. The `vary[]` system should include key transposition as a progression variable, not just shape and tempo.

3. **Tempo tiers should be substantive steps, not increments.** 60→80→100→120→140→160 BPM (or similar) creates real threshold crossings. Fine-grained increments don't.

4. **Approach patterns as sequence variation.** SlopScale's SEQUENCE_PATTERNS already implements this. The Aebersold/Guitar Method sources confirm that 1-2-3, 3-2-1, 1-1-1, 4-note variants are the most common and musically natural. Mixed approaches (combining two patterns) should be a "Next Variation" option.

5. **Regular review should be structural, not optional.** If a pathway never returns to earlier shapes/positions, it violates Leavitt's core principle. The "Next Variation" cycling should guarantee earlier shapes appear regularly even after new ones are introduced.

6. **Song context as the long-term motivator.** Aebersold and Serna both emphasize that exercises only stick when tied to real music. SlopScale could add a "song suggestion" display on pathway screens — e.g., "This scale is used in [song] — try it there after this drill."

7. **The student should improvise over the exercise backing.** The current architecture launches into the main Slopsmith player, which provides backing audio. This is exactly what Aebersold recommends (rhythm-section backing as soon as possible). The "Play" flow is correct; maintain it as the primary UX.

8. **The Aebersold 12-step procedure maps onto a pathway level system.** SlopScale could surface this as an explicit "Depth" setting: Level 1 = root only, Level 2 = root + 2 notes, ... Level 8 = full scale, Level 12 = broken thirds + arpeggios. Each level is a distinct exercise, not just the same exercise faster.

9. **Structured practice logs are a valuable feature analogue.** SlopScale doesn't have practice logs yet, but the Stefurak principle suggests the UI should encourage scheduling: "Practice this at [BPM] for [minutes] today." A simple "Set today's goal" prompt before launching the player would orient the session.

10. **Key cycling should be the default behavior, not an optional variation.** Aebersold, Ghijselen, and Leavitt all independently emphasize all-twelve-keys practice. SlopScale's `vary[]` key cycling should be presented as "the expected path," not as an advanced feature.

11. **Function family labels (Tonic/Subdominant/Dominant) make harmonic context legible.** When showing chord-scale exercises, adding the function label teaches the player *why* a particular scale sounds right over a chord — not just *that* it does. This is low-cost information display with high educational value.
